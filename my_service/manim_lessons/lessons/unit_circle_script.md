# Unit Circle — Narration Script
**Lesson file**: `unit_circle.py`
**Target length**: ~6 minutes (12th grade, 15 min focus)
**Audio language**: English

---

## Scene 1 (Hook) — about 40 seconds

`[title appears]`
> Quick question. What's sin 30°?

`[the three memorized values appear one by one]`
> Right — one half. And sin 45° is root 2 over 2. And sin 60° is root 3 over 2.

`[red question line at bottom]`
> You've memorized these. But why THESE numbers? Why not something else?

---

## Scene 2 (Define unit circle) — about 50 seconds

`[teach_concept block: Unit circle]`
> The answer starts with one picture — the unit circle.

`[circle drawn, r=1 label, equation]`
> A circle. Center at the origin. Radius one. That's it.
> Every point on it satisfies x squared plus y squared equals one.

---

## Scene 3 (sin/cos as coordinates) — about 90 seconds

`[radius line drawn from origin to circle]`
> Now pick a point on the circle. Draw a line from the origin to that point.

`[angle arc and θ label appear]`
> Call the angle between that line and the x-axis — theta.

`[x and y dashed lines drop to axes]`
> The point has a position. An x-coordinate. And a y-coordinate.

`[readout text appears at right]`
> And here's the entire trick of trigonometry:
> The x-coordinate IS cosine theta.
> The y-coordinate IS sine theta.

`[angle sweeps to 30°]`
> Watch. At 30 degrees, the y-coordinate is exactly one half.

`[angle sweeps to 45°]`
> At 45, it's root 2 over 2.

`[angle sweeps to 60°]`
> At 60, root 3 over 2.

> Those weren't magic numbers. They were just heights.

---

## Scene 4 (Misconception) — about 50 seconds

`[recall: sin 30° = 1/2]`
> So when your teacher said sin 30 equals one half...

`[misconception panel appears]`
> You probably thought: that's a number to memorize.

`[corrected panel appears]`
> Actually — it's a y-coordinate. The height of one specific point on one specific circle.
> Nothing to memorize. Just a height to read off.

---

## Scene 5 (Connect to graph) — about 100 seconds

`[heading "Where does the wave come from?"]`
> One more thing. You've seen the sine wave before — that smooth curve that goes up and down forever. Where does THAT come from?

`[two panels: circle on left, empty axes on right]`
> Same picture. We just record what the y-coordinate does as the angle sweeps around.

`[angle starts at 0, point traces along circle, wave starts drawing]`
> Angle equals zero. Point sits on the x-axis. Y-coordinate is zero. Plot that.

`[sweep continues to PI/2]`
> Sweep to 90 degrees. The y-coordinate climbs to one.

`[sweep continues, wave forms]`
> Keep going. The point goes around. The y-coordinate goes up, peaks, comes back down, goes negative, comes back up. And you've just drawn the sine wave.

`[label y = sin θ appears]`
> The wave isn't a separate thing. It's just the unit circle, unrolled.
> That's the connection to graphs you've been drawing: every point on a sine or cosine graph traces back to one point on this circle.

---

## Scene 6 (Radians) — about 70 seconds

`[teach_concept: Radians]`
> One last piece. Why do mathematicians say things like "pi over six" instead of "30 degrees"?

`[arc highlighted, length labeled]`
> Because on the unit circle, the arc length you travel IS the angle.
> A 30-degree arc has length pi over six. So we just call the angle "pi over six."

`[conversion list appears]`
> A full circle has circumference 2 pi — so a full turn is 2 pi radians.
> Half a turn? pi.
> Quarter turn? pi over two.

> No new ideas. Just a different name.

---

## Scene 7 (Framework) — about 40 seconds

`[framework appears in box]`
> So from now on, when you see sine or cosine of an angle — remember:

`[step 1]`
> Given an angle theta, the unit circle gives you a point — and the coordinates of that point are cosine theta and sine theta.

`[step 2]`
> Given a point on the circle, the angle is just how far you rotated to get there.

`[step 3]`
> Two directions. Same picture. No memorization.

---
